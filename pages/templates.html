📧 Module 03

Email Templates

Annotated sample emails illustrating common phishing characteristics for educational analysis.

⚠️ Training Material Only

All email templates below use fictional domains (e.g., example.com, training.local) and are clearly marked as simulation content. They must not be used outside of authorized training environments.

Template 1: Password Expiry Alert

From: IT Security Team <it-security@training-example.local>
To: trainee@training.local
Subject: ⚠️ URGENT: Your Password Expires in 24 Hours

Dear Employee,

Our records indicate that your network password will expire in 24 hours. To avoid losing access to your account, please reset your password immediately using the link below.

Reset My Password

If you do not reset your password within the next 24 hours, your account will be temporarily locked.

Thank you,
IT Security Team
training-example.local

🎓 TRAINING SIMULATION EMAIL — This is NOT a real email. Used for phishing awareness education only.
📝 Phishing Indicators to Identify

Urgency: "Expires in 24 hours" creates pressure to act quickly without thinking.

📝 Spoofed Sender

The "From" address uses a plausible but fictional domain. Real attackers often use domains that look similar to legitimate ones (e.g., examp1e.com).

📝 Generic Greeting

"Dear Employee" — legitimate IT communications typically address users by name.

📝 Threatening Consequences

"Account will be temporarily locked" — fear-based language pressures impulsive action.

Template 2: Shared Document Notification

From: Document Sharing <noreply@docs-training-example.local>
To: trainee@training.local
Subject: John Smith shared "Q4 Budget Report" with you

Hi there,

John Smith has shared a document with you:

📄 Q4 Budget Report.xlsx
Last modified: Today at 3:42 PM

Open Document

This link will expire in 7 days. If you were not expecting this document, please ignore this email.

🎓 TRAINING SIMULATION EMAIL — This is NOT a real email. Used for phishing awareness education only.
📝 Brand Impersonation

The email mimics the visual style of legitimate file-sharing notifications. The domain docs-training-example.local is designed to look plausible.

📝 Trust Exploitation

Using a colleague's name ("John Smith") makes the email feel familiar and trustworthy — a common social engineering technique.

Template 3: Account Suspicious Activity Alert

From: Security Alert <security@account-training-example.local>
To: trainee@training.local
Subject: 🔒 Unusual Sign-In Activity Detected on Your Account

We detected an unusual sign-in attempt on your account:

Location:Moscow, Russia
Device:Unknown Device — Linux
Time:Today at 2:17 AM

If this was you, you can ignore this message. If not, please secure your account immediately:

Secure My Account

This is an automated message from Account Security.

🎓 TRAINING SIMULATION EMAIL — This is NOT a real email. Used for phishing awareness education only.
📝 Fear-Based Manipulation

"Moscow, Russia" and "Unknown Device" are chosen to trigger fear and urgency. Real attackers use alarming details to bypass critical thinking.

📝 Defense Technique

Never click links in security alert emails. Instead, manually navigate to the service's website by typing the URL directly in your browser.

Key Takeaways

✅ What to Remember

All three templates demonstrate common phishing patterns: urgency, authority, fear, and trust exploitation. Recognizing these patterns is the first step in defense.
