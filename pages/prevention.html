Prevention & Awareness Guide

A comprehensive, practical guide to identifying, preventing, and reporting phishing attacks.

How to Identify Phishing Emails

Phishing emails exploit human psychology — urgency, fear, trust, and curiosity. Learning to recognize these patterns is your strongest defense.

The S.T.O.P. Method

✅ Before Acting on Any Email, S.T.O.P.

S — Sender: Do you recognize the sender? Does the email address match the claimed organization?
T — Tone: Is the email creating urgency, fear, or pressure to act immediately?
O — Objective: What is the email asking you to do? Click a link? Download a file? Share credentials?
P — Proof: Can you verify this request through an independent channel (phone call, official website)?

Common Red Flags

📧

Suspicious Sender Address

The display name says "IT Support" but the actual email is support@random-domain.xyz. Always check the full email address, not just the display name.

⏰

Urgency & Pressure

"Your account will be locked in 2 hours!" — Legitimate organizations rarely impose extreme urgency for routine actions like password resets.

🔗

Suspicious Links

Hover over links before clicking. The displayed text may say "login.example.com" but the actual URL points to login-examp1e.phish.xyz.

📎

Unexpected Attachments

Unsolicited attachments (especially .exe, .zip, .docm files) are a major red flag. Verify with the sender before opening any attachment.

✍️

Grammar & Spelling Errors

While not always present (AI has improved phishing quality), noticeable errors in professional communications are a warning sign.

👤

Generic Greetings

"Dear Customer" or "Dear User" instead of your actual name. Legitimate services that have your account usually address you by name.

Advanced Phishing Indicators

IndicatorHow to CheckWhat It Reveals
Email Headers View "Show Original" or "Message Source" in your email client SPF/DKIM/DMARC failures reveal spoofed senders
URL Shorteners Use URL expander tools to reveal the full destination Shortened URLs hide the true destination domain
Homograph Attacks Look carefully at domain names for character substitution e.g., using Cyrillic "а" instead of Latin "a" in domains
Subdomain Tricks Check the actual domain (rightmost before TLD) login.example.com.evil.xyz — the real domain is evil.xyz
QR Code Phishing Use a QR scanner that previews URLs before opening QR codes in emails can direct to phishing sites

Best Practices for Users

  • Never click links in unexpected emails — navigate to the site directly by typing the URL
  • Enable multi-factor authentication (MFA) on all accounts
  • Use a password manager to avoid entering credentials on unfamiliar sites
  • Verify requests through a separate channel (call, in-person, official app)
  • Keep your software and browser updated for the latest security patches
  • Report suspicious emails using your organization's reporting mechanism
  • Never share passwords, MFA codes, or personal information via email
  • Be extra cautious with emails received on mobile devices (limited URL visibility)
  • Don't trust caller ID — voice phishing (vishing) can spoof phone numbers too
  • When in doubt, ask your IT/security team — it's always better to ask

Best Practices for Organizations

Technical Controls

  • Email Authentication: Implement SPF, DKIM, and DMARC to prevent email spoofing
  • Email Filtering: Deploy advanced email security gateways with sandboxing
  • URL Filtering: Block known malicious domains at the DNS/proxy level
  • MFA Enforcement: Require MFA for all user accounts, especially privileged ones
  • Browser Isolation: Use remote browser isolation for risky web browsing
  • Endpoint Protection: Deploy EDR solutions for real-time threat detection

Process Controls

  • Phishing Report Button: Install a one-click reporting plugin in all email clients
  • Verification Procedures: Establish out-of-band verification for financial requests
  • Incident Response Plan: Define clear steps for when a phishing email is reported or clicked
  • Access Reviews: Regularly review user access and privileges

Reporting Procedures

📋 How to Report a Suspected Phishing Email

Step 1: Do NOT click any links, download attachments, or reply to the email.
Step 2: Use your email client's "Report Phishing" button (if available).
Step 3: Forward the email as an attachment to your security team (e.g., security@yourorg.example).
Step 4: If you already clicked a link or entered credentials, contact IT security immediately and change your passwords.
Step 5: Document what happened — when you received the email, what you did, and any details you noticed.

Building a Security Culture

🏆

Reward Reporting

Recognize and reward users who report phishing attempts. Create a "Phishing Champions" program to celebrate security-aware behavior.

🚫

No Blame Culture

Never punish users for falling for simulations. A blame-free environment encourages honest reporting and faster incident response.

📅

Regular Training

Conduct quarterly simulations and annual refresher training. Consistent exposure builds lasting security habits and muscle memory.

👥

Leadership Buy-In

Security culture starts at the top. When leadership participates in training and champions security, the entire organization follows.

Cultural Improvement Tips

  • Make it personal: Show employees how phishing affects them personally (identity theft, financial loss), not just the company
  • Gamify awareness: Use leaderboards, badges, and team competitions to make security training engaging
  • Share real examples: Regularly share anonymized examples of phishing attempts targeting your industry
  • Micro-learning: Deliver short, frequent training bursts (2-3 minutes) rather than long annual sessions
  • Measure and communicate: Share improvement metrics with the organization to demonstrate progress
  • Include onboarding: Make security awareness part of new employee orientation from day one
💡 Remember

Security is everyone's responsibility. The most sophisticated technical defenses can be bypassed by a single click. An aware and vigilant workforce is the most effective defense against phishing.
