4. User Behavior Insights

Key Findings

  • Urgency is the most effective trigger: Password expiry scenarios had the highest click-through rate (45%) compared to document sharing (32%) and BEC (18%)
  • Training has measurable impact: The 75% reduction in CTR between Campaign 1 and 3 demonstrates that structured awareness training significantly improves user behavior
  • Reporting behavior is trainable: The 675% increase in reporting rate shows that users can be effectively taught to report suspicious emails
  • Repeated exposure builds resilience: Each subsequent campaign showed improvement, suggesting that regular simulation exercises reinforce learning
  • Time-of-day matters: Emails sent at the start of the workday (9-10 AM) had higher click rates than those sent mid-afternoon

Behavioral Patterns Observed

⚡

Impulsive Clickers

~15% of users clicked within 30 seconds of opening the email, indicating reflexive behavior without analysis. This group benefited most from training.

🤔

Cautious Reviewers

~40% of users who opened the email did not click. Post-survey data showed they noticed at least one red flag before deciding not to interact.

🛡️

Active Reporters

The reporting cohort grew from 8% to 62% across campaigns. Many reporters also helped warn colleagues, creating a positive peer-influence effect.

6. Conclusion & Recommendations

✅ Report Conclusion Template

Summary: The phishing simulation exercise demonstrated that structured security awareness training significantly reduces susceptibility to phishing attacks. Key metrics showed a 75% reduction in click-through rates and a 675% increase in reporting rates over three campaigns.

Top 3 Recommendations:
1. Implement regular (quarterly) phishing simulation exercises
2. Deploy technical controls (MFA, DMARC, URL filtering) to complement training
3. Foster a positive security culture where reporting is encouraged and rewarded
