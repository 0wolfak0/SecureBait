Simulation Design

Training-only phishing scenarios that demonstrate real-world attack patterns safely.

ℹ️ Purpose

These scenarios are designed to educate users on recognizing phishing — not to trick them. Every simulation includes post-interaction training and immediate feedback.

Scenario 1: Fake Password Reset Alert

Threat Model

An attacker sends an email impersonating an IT department, claiming the user's password is expiring. The email creates urgency to pressure the user into clicking a malicious link and entering credentials on a fake login page.

What the Simulation Demonstrates

  • Social Engineering Tactic: Creating urgency ("Your password expires in 24 hours")
  • Spoofed Sender: Email appears to come from it-security@training-example.local
  • Deceptive Link: The "Reset Password" button points to a training awareness page, not a real login
  • Visual Mimicry: The email uses corporate-looking branding to appear legitimate

Learning Outcomes

  • Recognize urgency-based social engineering tactics
  • Verify sender addresses before taking action
  • Hover over links to check actual destination URLs
  • Use official channels to verify password reset requests
  • Report suspicious emails using the organization's reporting mechanism

Scenario 2: Fake Shared Document Notification

Threat Model

An attacker sends an email mimicking a file-sharing service notification (e.g., "John shared a document with you"). The goal is to trick the user into clicking a link and entering their credentials on a fake login page.

What the Simulation Demonstrates

  • Brand Impersonation: Email mimics the look of legitimate file-sharing notifications
  • Trust Exploitation: Uses a colleague's name to build trust
  • Credential Harvesting Pattern: Redirects to a fake login that mimics a popular service
  • URL Mismatch: Display URL differs from actual destination

Scenario 3: CEO/Authority Impersonation (BEC)

Threat Model

Business Email Compromise (BEC) — an attacker impersonates a senior executive to request an urgent action, such as processing a payment or sharing sensitive data.

What the Simulation Demonstrates

  • Authority Pressure: The email claims to be from the CEO/Director
  • Urgency + Secrecy: "Handle this privately and urgently"
  • Process Bypass: Asks the user to skip normal procedures
  • No Attachments/Links: BEC often relies purely on social engineering text
💡 Training Tip

BEC attacks are among the most costly forms of phishing. They often don't contain links or malware — just persuasive text. Train users to verify requests through a separate communication channel (e.g., call the executive directly).

Post-Simulation Training Framework

Every simulation scenario should include immediate educational feedback:

  1. Awareness Page: Users who click are shown an educational page explaining what happened
  2. Micro-Training Module: A 2-3 minute interactive lesson on the specific attack type
  3. Quiz/Knowledge Check: 3-5 questions to reinforce learning
  4. Resource Links: Additional reading and official reporting procedures
  5. No Shaming: The tone must be supportive and educational, never punitive
✅ Ethical Principle

The goal is awareness improvement, not catching people out. Simulations should empower users with knowledge, not create a culture of fear.
